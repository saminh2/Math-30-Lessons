Lesson 4: Solving Polynomial Equations

King Tutankhamun's Tomb: Tutankhamun was born about 1343 B.C. He became king of ancient Egypt when he was only nine years old. When the Tutankhamun's tomb was discovered, it contained hundreds of objects. The valuables, such as gold statues, had been buried in the boxes. One of the boxes, in the shape of a rectangular prism, had a volume of about 45 m3. The width and the height of the box were approximately equal, and the length of the box was 2 m greater than the width.

The volume, `V(x)`, of the box, where `x` is the width, can be expressed as follows.

`V=l\times w\times h`
`V(x)=(x+2)\times x\times x`
`=x^2(x+2)`
`=x^3+2x^2`

The volume was 45 m3, so

`x^3+2x^2=45`
or `x^3+2x^2-45=0`

The equation `x^3+2x^2-45=0` is an example of a polynomial equation, also known as a cubic or third-degree equation.

In this lesson you will solve polynomial equations:

  • You can use the factor theorem and the integral zero theorem to solve polynomial equations by factoring.
  • You can solve polynomial equations by graphing the corresponding function. The `x`-intercepts of the corresponding function provide the solutions to the equation.
  • The shape of the graph of a function close to a zero depends on its multiplicity.
  • Not all functions change sing at a real zero.

The Factor Theorem and Integral Zero Theorem Summary

  • A polynomial equation is an equation in which a polynomial is set equal to 0 or to another polynomial.

  • You can sketch the graph of a polynomial function using the `x`-intercepts, the `y`-intercept, the degree of the function, and the sign of the leading coefficient.

  • Polynomial equations can be solved by factoring or by graphing.

    • The `x`-intercepts of the graph of a polynomial function are the roots of the corresponding polynomial equation.
    • When a polynomial function is in factored form, you can determine the zeros from the factors. When it is not in factored form, you can use the factor theorem and the integral zero theorem to determine the factors.

  • When a factor is repeated `n` times, the corresponding zero has multiplicity, `n`.

    • Polynomial functions change sign at `x`-intercepts that correspond to zeros of odd multiplicity.
    • Polynomial functions do not change sign at `x`-intercepts that correspond to zeros of even multiplicity.

  • The shape of a graph close to a zero of `x=a` (multiplicity `n`) is similar to the shape of the graph of a function with degree equal to `n` of the form `y=(x-a)^n`.

    • A curve that cuts or crosses the `x`-axis has a multiplicity of one.

    • A graph that is tangent or bounces off the `x`-axis will always have a multiplicity of 2.

    • A curve that meanders across or gently sits while crossing the `x`-axis will have a multiplicity of 3.

  • The graph of a polynomial function of the form `y=a[b(x-h)]^n +k`   or   `y-k=a[b(x-h)]^n` can be sketched by applying transformations to the graph of `y=x^n`, where `n\in N`.

Polynomial Equation with One Side Equal to Zero.

The following values are identical when given a polynomial equation with one side equal to zero:

  • the zeros of the polynomial
  • the roots of the equation
  • the `x`-intercepts of the graph of the polynomial

Having zero on one side of the equation is crucial. If the equation had been something like `(2x-3)(x+1)(x-2)=7` (note the 7 instead of 0), the zeros of the polynomial on the left side `(x=3/2, -1, 2)` would not be the same as the root of the equation `(x=2.7481...)`.

Example 1

Determine the roots of the following polynomial equation, `y=x^4-6x^3+12x^2-8x`

Solution

`y=x^4-6x^3+12x^2-8x`

`y=x(x^3-6x^2+12x-8)`

`y=x(x-2)(x-4x+4)`

`y=x((x-2)(x-2)(x-2)`

`y=x(x-2)^3`

  • The graph of a function with a zero `x=2` of multiplicity 3 looks like the graph of the 3-rd degree function `y=(x-2)^3` in the region close to `x= 2`.
  • The graph crosses over the `x`-axis at the zero of odd multiplicity.
  • The function is positive for values of `x` in the intervals `x\lt 0` and `x\gt 2`. The function is negative for values of `x` in the interval `0\lt x\lt 2`.
  • The polynomial equation `x^4-6x^3+12x^2-8x=0` has two distinct real roots `x=0` and `x=2`.

Example 2

Determine the roots of the following polynomial equation, `y=x^5-8x^4+24x^3-32x^2+16x`

Solution

`y=x^5-8x^4+24x^3-32x^2+16x`

`y=x(x^4-8x^3+24x^2-32x+16)`

`y=x(x-2)(x^3-6x^2+12x-8)`

`y=x(x-2)(x-2)(x^2-4x+4)`

`y=x(x-2)(x-2)(x-2)(x-2)`

`y=x(x-2)^4`

  • The graph of a function with a zero of `x=2` of multiplicity 4 looks like the graph of the 4-th degree function `y=(x-2)^4` in the region close to `x=2`.
  • The graph touches, but does not cross, the `x`-axis at the zero of even multiplicity.
  • The function is positive for values of `x` in the intervals `0 \lt x\lt 2` and `x\gt 2`. The function is negative for values of `x` in the interval `x\lt 0`.
  • The polynomial equation `x^5-8x^4+24x^3-32x^2+16x=0` has two distinct real roots `x=0` and `x=2`.

Example 3

Solve each equation.

  1. `(x+1)(x-4)(x+5)=0`
  2. `(x+6)(x-3)^2=0`
  3. `x^3+x^2=6x`
  4. `0=w^3-5w^2+2w+8`

Solution

  1. `(x-1)(x-4)(x+5)=0`

    Set each factor equal to `0`.

    `(x+1)=0, x=-1`

    `(x-4)=0, x=4`

    `(x+5)=0, x=-5`

    The solutions are -5, -1, and 4.

  2. `(x+6)(x-3)^2=0`

    `(x+6)(x-3)(x=3)=0`

    Set each factor to `0`.

    `(x+6)=0, x=-6`

    `(x-3)=0, x=3`

    `(x-3)=0, x=3`

    The solutions are -6 and 3.

  3. Rewrite `x^3+x^2=6x` as `x^3+x^2-6x=0`

    `x^3+x^2-6x=0`

    `x(x^2+x-6)=0`

    `x(x+3)(x-2)=0`

    Set each factor to `0`.

    `x=0`

    `(x+3)=0, x=-3`

    `(x-2)=0, x=2`

    The solutions are -3, 0, and 2.

  4. `w^3-5w^2+2w+8=0`

    `P(-1)=0`

    Therefore, `(w+1)` is a factor.

    Use synthetic division to find another factor.

    `(w+1)(w^2-6w+8)=0`

    `(w+1)(w-2)(w-4)=0`

    Set each factor to `0`.

    `(w+1)=0, w=-1`

    `(w-2)=0, w=2`

    `(w-4)=0, w=4`

    The solutions are -1, 2, and 4.

Example 4

Given the graph of a polynomial function, find solutions of the corresponding polynomial equation. The roots are all integral values.

  1. `x^3+5x^2-2x-24=0`

  2. `x^3-2x^2-4x+8=0`

  3. `-x^4-2x^3+8x^2+18x+9=0`

  4. `x^4+x^3-3x^2-5x-2=0`

Solution

  1. The `x`-intercepts appear to be -4, -3, and 2.

    The solutions are -4, -3, and 2.

  2. The `x`-intercepts appear to be -2 and 2.

    The solutions are -2 and 2.

  3. The `x`-intercepts appear to be -3, -1, and 3.

    The solutions are -3, -1, and 3.

  4. The `x`-intercepts appear to be -1 and 2.

    The solutions are -1 and 2.

Example 5

For the following graph of a polynomial function,

  1. State the `x`-intercepts.
  2. What is the sign of the leading coefficient?
  3. Fill in the blanks the intervals and select where the function is positive or negative.
    4. Interval `x<-3` `-3 < x < 2` `x>2`
    Sign of `f(x)`

  4. Explain whether the graph might represent a polynomial that has zero(s) of multiplicity 1, 2, or 3.

  5. Write the corresponding polynomial possible equation.

  6. State the roots of the equation from e).

Solution

  1. The `x`-intercepts are -3 and 2.

  2. The graph extends up into quadrant I and up into quadrant II, so the leading coefficient is positive.

  3. Interval `x<-3` `-3 < x < 2` `x>2`
    Sign of `f(x)` positive negative positive

  4. The graph crosses the `x`-axis at both `x`-intercepts, so the two `x`-intercepts are of odd multiplicity. The least possible multiplicity is 1 and the greatest possible multiplicity is 3. In the neighborhood of `x=2`, the graph behaves like the cubic curve `y=(x-2)^3`. Therefore, `x=-3` is the zero of multiplicity 1 and `x=2` is a zero of multiplicity 3.

  5. The `x`-intercepts are -3 and 2 (multiplicity 3). The factors are `x+3`, and `(x-2)^3`.

    The possible polynomial equation is `(x+3)(x-2)^3=0`

  6. `(x+3)(x-2)^3=0`

    Set each factor to `0`.

    `(x+3)=0, x=-3`

    `(x-2)=0, x=2`

    The solutions are -3 and 2. The `x`-intercepts of the graph of a polynomial function are the roots of the corresponding polynomial equation.

Example 6

Sketch the graph of `y=-2(1/4x-1)^3+5` using transformations. State the domain and range of the function.

Solution

For the function as `y=-2(1/4x-1)^3+5`, factor the coefficient of the `x` term to identify the transformations of the function more easily. So, rewrite `y=-2(1/4x-1)^3+5` as `y=-2(1/4(x-4))^3+5`.

To sketch the graph of `y=-2(1/4(x-4))^3+5`, first sketch the graph of `y=x^3`. Then, sketch the graph of `y=2x^3` which is a vertical stretch of the graph of `y=x^3` by a factor of 2. Then, sketch the graph of `y=2(1/4x)^3` which is a horizontal stretch of the graph of `y=2x^3` by a factor of 4.

Then, sketch the graph of `y=-2(1/4x)^3` which is a reflection of the graph of `y=2(1/4x)^3` in the `x`-axis.

Then, apply the horizontal translation of 4 units to the right and the vertical translation of 5 units upward.

The result is the graph of `y=-2(1/4(x-4))^3+5` or `y=-2(1/4x-1)^3+5`.

The domain is `{x|x\ in R}` and the range is `{y|y\ in R}`.

Example 7

Solve the equation `x^3+2x^2-45=0` to find the dimensions of one of the boxes found in Tutankhamun's tomb, where the width and the height of the box are each `x` metres, and the length is `x+2` metres.

Solution

The factors of -45 are `\pm1`, `\pm3`, `\pm5`, `\pm9`, `\pm15`, and `\pm45`.

`P(1)=-42`        `P(3)=0`         `P(-1)=-44`

Since `P(3)=0`, `x-3` is a factor of `x^3+2x^2-45`.

Use synthetic division to find another factor.

Another factor is `x^2+5x+15`.

Use the quadratic formula with `a=1`, `b=5`, and `c=15` to check for other real roots.

`x=(-b+-sqrt(b^2-4ac))/(2a)`

`x=(-5+-sqrt((5)^2-4(1)(15)))/(2(1))`

`x=(-5+-sqrt(25-60))/2`

`x=(-5+-sqrt(-35))/2`

Since the square root of a negative number is not a real number, there are no real roots.

Therefore, `x^2+5x+15` cannot be factored.

So, `x=3`. The width and the height of the box are each 3 m, and the length is 5 m.
