Lesson 3: The Factor Theorem

Moai Statues: Easter Island, also known as Rapa Nui, is a tiny volcanic island in the Pacific Ocean with a mysterious history and some really big statues. The statues consist of large heads with elongated noses and ears.

Several statues were carved from prisms whose volumes can be represented by the expression `x^3-9x^2+20x`, where `x` metres is the height of each prism. Factoring the expression `x^3-9x^2+20x` will give the other dimensions of each prism.

There are two theorems covered in this lesson:

  • The factor theorem is a special case of the remainder theorem. It is used in the same manner as the remainder theorem, but when the remainder is zero, the divisor is considered a factor of the polynomial.
  • The integral zero theorem provides a way to make a short list of all the possible integers that could make a polynomial equal to zero.
  • You can use the factors of the constant term of the polynomial to look for factors of the polynomial.

The Factor Theorem and Integral Zero Theorem Summary

  • The factor theorem can be stated as follows.

    A polynomial `P(x)` has `x-a` as a factor if and only if `P(a)=0`.

  • "If and only if" means that the result works both ways. So, the factor theorem can be re-formulated in two statements:
    • If `x-a` is a factor of `P(x)`, then `P(a)=0`.
    • If `P(a)=0`, then `x-a` is a factor of `P(x)`.

  • The integral zero theorem provides a way to know which values of `a` to try when attempting to factor a polynomial. The factor theorem can be expressed as follows.

    If `x-a` is a factor of a polynomial function `P(x)` with integral coefficients, then `a` is a factor of the constant term of `P(x)`.

  • The integral zero of a polynomial, `P(x)`, is the integer value of `x` that makes the polynomial equal to 0.

Important! Factors of Two Opposite Numbers

The factors of two opposite numbers are the same. For example, the factors of 10 and -10 are the same: `\pm1`, `\pm2`, `\pm5`, and `\pm10`.

Example 1

Factor `x^3-x^2-17x-15`.

Solution

Step 1: Find a factor by evaluating `P(x)` for values of `x` that are the factors of the constant term, -15.

The factors of -15 are `\pm1`, `\pm3`, `\pm5`, and `\pm15`.

Step 2: Apply the factor theorem to determine one factor.

`P(x)=x^3-x^2-17x-15`

`P(1)=1^3-1^2-17(1)-15`

`P(1)=1-1-17-15`

`P(1)=-32`

Since `P(1) ne 0`, then `x-1` is not a factor of the polynomial. Try the next factor.

`P(-1)=(-1)^3-(-1)^2-17(-1)-15`

`P(-1)=-1-1+17-15`

`P(-1)=0`

Since `P(-1)=0`, `x+1` is a factor of `x^3-x^2-17x-15`.

Step 3: Use either long or synthetic division to find another factor.

Another factor is `x^2-2x-15`.

Step 4: Repeat the above steps until all factors are found or the remaining factor is a trinomial which can be factored.

Factoring `x^2-2x-15` gives `(x-5)(x+3)`.

So, `x^3-x^2-17x-15=(x-5)(x+3)(x+1)`.

Important! Graphing the Corresponding Function

The factors of the polynomial `x^3-x^2-17x-15` could have been founded by graphing the corresponding function `P(x)=x^3-x^2-17x-15`.

The `x`-intercepts appear to be 5, -3, and -1. These are the real zeros of the function. Using these zeros and the zero product property, the polynomial can be written in factored form, as follows.

`P(x)=(x-5)(x+3)(x+1)`

Example 2

Factor `x^3-1`.

Solution

The factors of 1 are `\pm1`.

`P(x)=x^3-1`

`P(1)=1^3-1`

`P(1)=1-1`

`P(1)=0`

Therefore, `x-1` is a factor of `x^3-1`.

Use synthetic division to find another factor.

Note that `x^3-1` can be written as `x^3+0x^2+0x-1`.

Another factor of `x^3-1` is `x^2+x+1`.

The expression `x^2+x+1` cannot be factored.

So, `x^3-1=(x-1)(x^2+x+1)`.

Example 3

Use the factor theorem to determine whether each polynomial has a factor of `x-4`.

  1. `3x^2-7x-20`
  2. `x^3-5x^2+3x+4`
  3. `x^4-2x^3-8x^2+3x-4`
  4. `x^4-3x^3-5x^2+16`

Solution

  1. Yes.

    `P(4)=3(4)^2-7(4)-20`

    `P(4)=48-28-20`

    `P(4)=0`

    Since `P(4)=0`, `x-4` is a factor of `3x^2-7x-20`.

  2. Yes.

    `P(4)=(4)^3-5(4)^2+3(4)+4`

    `P(4)=64-80+12+4`

    `P(4)=0`

    Since `P(4)=0`, `x-4` is a factor of `x^3-5x^2+3x+4`.

  3. No.

    `P(4)=(4)^4-2(4)^3-8(4)^2+3(4)-4`

    `P(4)=256-128-128+12-4`

    `P(4)=8`

    Since `P(4)=8`, `x-4` is not factor of `x^4-2x^3-8x^2+3x-4`.

  4. Yes.

    `P(4)=(4)^4-3(4)^3-5(4)^2+16`

    `P(4)=256-192-80+16`

    Since `P(4)=0`, `x-4` is a factor of `x^4-3x^3-5x^2+16`

Example 4

Factor each polynomial completetly.

  1. `x^3-2x^2-6x-8`
  2. `k^3+6k^2-7k-60`
  3. `x^3+27`
  4. `2w^4+3w^3-7w^2-12w-4`

Solution

  1. The factors of -8 are `\pm1`, `\pm2`, `\pm4`, and `\pm8`.

    `P(1)=-15`      `P(2)=20`      `P(4)=0`      `P(-1)=-5`      `P(-2)=-12`

    Since `P(4)=0`, `x-4` is a factor of `x^3-2x^2-6x-8`.

    Use synthetic division to find another factor.

    Another factor is `x^2+2x+2`.

    The expression `x^2+2x+2` cannot be factored.

    So, `x^3-2x^2-6x-8=(x-4)(x^2+2x+2)`.

  2. The factors of -60 are `\pm1`, `\pm2`, `\pm3`, `\pm4`, `\pm5`, `\pm6`, `\pm10`, `\pm12`, `\pm15`, `\pm20`, `\pm30`, and `\pm60`.

    `P(1)=60`       `P(2)=-46`        `P(4)=72`       `P(-1)=-48`        `P(-2)=-30`       `P(-4)=0`

    Since `P(-4)=0`, `k+4` is a factor of `k^3+6k^2-7x-60`.

    Use synthetic division to find another factor.

    Another factor is `k^2+2k-15`.

    Factoring `k^2+2k-15` gives `(k-3)(k+5)`.

    So, `k^3+6k^2-7x-60=(k-3)(k+4)(k+5)`.

  3. The factors of 27 are `\pm1`, `\pm3`, `\pm9`, and `\pm27`.

    `P(1)=28`        `P(3)=54`
    `P(-1)=26`      `P(-3)=0`

    Since `P(-3)=0`, `x+3` is a factor of `x^3+27`.

    Use synthetic division to find another factor.

    Another factor is `x^2-3x+9`.

    The expression `x^2-3x+9` cannot be factored.

    So, `x^3+27 =(x+3)(x^2-3x+9)`.

  4. The factors of -4 are `\pm1`, `\pm2`, and `\pm4`.

    `P(1)=-18`
    `P(-1)=0`

    Since `P(-1)=0`, `w+1` is a factor of `2w^4+3w^3-7w^2-12w-4`.

    Use synthetic division to find another factor.

    Another factor is `2w^3+w^2-8w-4`.

    Use the factor theorem again. The factors of -4 are `\pm1`, `\pm2`, and `\pm4`.

    `P(1)=-9`        `P(2)=0`         `P(-1)=3`

    Since `P(2)=0`, `w-2` is a factor of `2w^3+w^2-8w-4`.

    Use synthetic division to find another factor.

    Another factor is `2w^2+5w+2`.

    Factor `2w^2+5w+2` by grouping.

    `2w^2+5w+2`

    `2w^2+4w+w+2`

    `2w(w+2)+(w+2)`

    `(w+2)(2w+1)`

    So, `2w^4+3w^3-7w^2-12w-4=(w-2)(w+1)(w+2)(2w+1)`.

Example 5

Given a polynomial, `P(x)`, how many of the following statements are correct?

  1. The possible integral zeros of `P(x)` consist of all the factors of the constant term.
  2. If `P(a)=0`, then `a` is a factor of the constant term of `P(x)`.
  3. If `a` is a factor of the constant term of `P(x)`, then `x-a` is a factor of `P(x)`.
  4. If `x-a` is a factor of `P(x)`, then `P(a)=0`.
  5. If `P(a)=0`, then `x-a` is a factor of `P(x)`.
  6. If `x-a` is a factor of `P(x)`, then `a` is a factor of the constant term of `P(x)`.

Solution

Five of the above statements are correct.

Statement 3 is not correct. Not all of the factors of the constant term may satisfy the equation `P(x)=0`. The factor theorem states that a polynomial `P(x)` has `x-a` as a factor if and only if `P(a)=0`.

Example 6

Return to the Moai Statues problem from the introduction section of this lesson.

  1. Factor the expression `x^3-9x^2+20x` to find the dimensions of the rectangular prisms used for some of the statues on Easter Island.
  2. Use the factor theorem to check your answers to part a).
  3. If `x` represents 6 m, what were the dimensions of the rectangular prism?

Solution

  1. `x^3-9x^2+20x=x(x^2-9x+20)`

      `=x(x-5)(x-4)`

    The dimensions are `x`, `x-5`, and `x-4` metres.

  2. `P(0)=0^3-9(0)^2+20(0)`

    `P(0)=0=0+0`

    `P(0)=0`

    Note that factor `x` can be written as `x-0`.

    `P(5)=5^3-9(5)^2+20(5)`

    `P(5)=125-225+100`

    `P(5)=0`

     

    `P(4)=4^3-9(4)^2+20(4)`

    `P(4)=64-144+80`

    `P(4)=0`

  3. `x=6` m

    `x-5=6-5=1` m

    `x-4=6-4=2` m

    If `x=6` m, the dimensions are 1 m, 2 m, and 6 m.
