Lesson 1: Characteristics of Polynomial Functions

Many real-life situations can be modeled by polynomial functions. For example, the volume of a silo is a polynomial function of its radius.

In this lesson you will investigate the following properties of polynomial functions:

  • A polynomial function is an equation of the form `f(x)=a_(n)x^n+a_(n-1)x^(n-1)+...+a_(2)x^2+a_(1)x+a_(0)`, where the coefficients `a_n`, `a_(n-1)`, ..., `a_1`, `a_0` represent real numbers, `a_n` is not zero, and the exponents are non-negative integers.
  • The real zeros of a function are the `x`-intercepts of its graph.
  • You can describe the behavior of a function based on its degree, the leading coefficient and the constant term.

Notice that a function CANNOT be classified as a polynomial function if:

  • an exponent is a fraction, negative number, radical, etc. All exponents must be a positive integers.
  • a variable is in the denominator. All variables must exist in the numerators only.
  • a variable is located within a root sign

Let's look at a specific example to cover some important terms:

Example 1

`f(x)=2x^4+3x^3-x^2-5x+10`.

  • The coefficients are 2, 3, -1, -5 and 10. They can be any real number.
  • The exponents are 4, 3, 2 and 1. They must be positive integers.
  • The degree of the polynomial is 4. This is the highest exponent on a variable. The terms should always be arranged such that the exponents are in descending order.
  • The leading coefficient is 2. It is the coefficient of the highest degree term.
  • The constant term is 10. It is the term without a variable, or a variable of degree zero (`x^0`).

Polynomial Functions Summary

  • A polynomial function is an equation of the form `f(x)=a_(n)x^n+a_(n-1)x^(n-1)+...+a_(2)x^2+a_(1)x+a_(0)`, where the coefficients `a_n`, `a_(n-1)`, ..., `a_1`, `a_0` represent real numbers, `a_n` is not zero, and the exponents are non-negative integers.

  • The degree of a polynomial function is equal to the highest exponent of the variable.

  • When a polynomial is written in expanded form and in order of decreasing exponents, the first term is called the leading term. The first term’s coefficient is called the leading coefficient.

  • Graphs of odd-degree polynomial functions have the following characteristics:

    • When the leading coefficient is positive, a graph extends up into quadrant I and down into quadrant III.
    • When the leading coefficient is negative, a graph extends up into quadrant II and down into quadrant IV.
    • There can be from one to up to a maximum of `n` `x`-intercepts, where `n` is the degree of the function. There is at least one `x`-intercept.
    • A `y`-intercept corresponds to the constant term of the function.
    • A domain is `{x|x\ in R}` and a range is `{y|yinR}`.
    • There are no maximum or minimum points.

  • Graphs of even-degree polynomial functions have the following characteristics:

    • When the leading coefficient is positive, a graph extends up into quadrant I and up into quadrant II; and there is a minimum point.
    • When the leading coefficient is negative, a graph extends down into quadrant III and down into quadrant IV; and there is a maximum point.
    • There can be from zero to up to a maximum of `n` `x`-intercepts, where `n` is the degree of the function.
    • A `y`-intercept corresponds to the constant term of the function.
    • A domain is `{x|x\ in R}` and a range depends on the maximum or minimum value of the function.
    • A zero-degree polynomial function is called a constant function. Its graph is a horizontal line that has zero `x`-intercepts.

End Behaviour

When sketching polynomial functions, one of the characteristics of interest is the behaviour of `f(x)` (the `y`-coordinates) as `x` approaches infinity or negative infinity. This is called end behaviour. Consider the following graph:

As `x` approaches `-\infty` (negative infinity), the `y`-coordinates approach `\infty` (infinity or positive infinity).
As `x` approaches `\infty`, the `y`-coordinates approach `-\infty`. So, you would say that this graph extends up into quadrant II and down into quadrant IV.

The end behaviour of all odd-degree polynomial functions is the same as `y=x` or `y=-x`. Since the graphs of `y=x` and `y= -x` are easy to remember, so is the end behaviour of all odd functions.

The end behaviour of all even-degree polynomial functions is the same as `y=x^2` or `y=-x^2`. Since the graphs of `y=x^2` and `y=-x^2` are easy to remember, so is the end behaviour of all even functions.

    Substituting zero for `x` gives the `y`-intercept (0, 5).

  3. The degree of the polynomial is even and the polynomial has a negative leading coefficient. The graph will extend down into quadrants III and IV.

    Substituting zero for `x` gives the `y`-intercept (0, 4).

  4. The degree of the polynomial is odd and the polynomial has a positive leading coefficient. The graph will extend down into quadrant III and extend up into quadrant I.

    Substituting zero for `x` gives the `y`-intercept (0, 0).

    Substituting zero for `x` gives the `y`-intercept (0, 4).

    Substituting zero for `x` gives the `y`-intercept (0, -18).

Solution

  2. Where `0 < x < 5`

  3. Using technology, the volume is a maximum when `x\approx 2.23` cm

  4. Using technology, the maximum volume of the box is 192 cm^3.
