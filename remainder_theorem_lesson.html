Lesson 2: The Remainder Theorem

A digital signature is basically a way to ensure that an electronic document is authentic, meaning that you know who created the document and you know that it has not been altered in any way since that person created it. It relies on the encryption techniques that use polynomial division.

In this lesson you will investigate the remainder theorem:

  • The main task is to discover a shortcut for finding the remainder when you divide a polynomial by a binomial in the form `(x-a)`. This shortcut is called the remainder theorem.
  • If `P(x)` is divided by `(x-a)`, then `P(a)` is the remainder.
  • You can use long division or synthetic division as an alternative to divide a polynomial by a binomial.

The Remainder Theorem Summary

  • If a polynomial `P(x)` is divided by `x-a, ainI`, and the division is continued until the remainder is a constant `R`, then

Dividend = (Divisor)(Quotient) + Remainder

  • If the quotient is `Q(x)`, the following division statement is true for all values of `x`.

      `P(x)=(x-a)xxQ(x)+R`
    Substitute `a` for `x`: `P(a)=(a-a)xxQ(a)+R`
    Simplify: `P(a)=0xxQ(a)+R`
      `P(a)=R`

    The relationship between `P(a)` and `R` is expressed in the remainder theorem, which can be stated as follows.

    When a polynomial `P(x)` is divided by `x-a, ainI`, and the remainder is a constant, then the remainder is `P(a)`.

  • The result of this division may be written as follows:

    `(P(x))/(x-a)=Q(x)+R/(x-a)`

    or

    `P(x)=(x-a)Q(x)+R`

  • Synthetic division is a method of performing polynomial long division involving a binomial divisor that uses only the coefficients of the terms and fewer calculations.

Example 1

  1. Find the remainder when the polynomial `P(x)=2x^3-4x^2+ 3x-6` is divided by `x+2`.
  2. Divide the polynomial `P(x)=2x^3-4x^2+3x-6` by `x+2`. Express the result in the form `(P(x))/(x-a)=Q(x)+R/(x-a)`.
  3. Identify any restrictions on the variable.
  4. Check the division by multiplication and addition and write the corresponding statement.

Solution

  1. The remainder theorem states that, when `P(x)=2x^3-4x^2+ 3x-6` is divided by `x+2`, the remainder is `P(-2)`.

    `P(-2)=2(-2)^3-4(-2)^2+ 3(-2)-6`
    `=-16-16-6-6`
    `=-44`

    The remainder is `-44`.

  2. Use long division.

    The result can be written in the form of division statement.

    `(2x^3-4x^2+3x-6)/(x+2)=2x^2-8x+19+(-44)/(x+2)`

  3. Since division by zero is not defined, the divisor cannot be zero: `x+2\ne 0` or `x\ne -2`.

  4. Multiply the divisor by the quotient and add the remainder to check the division.

    The corresponding statement is: `2x^3-4x^2+ 3x-6=(x+2)(2x^2-8x+19)-44`

Example 2: Synthetic Division

  1. Use synthetic division to divide `x^3-6x^2+7x+6` by `x-3`. Identify any restrictions on the variable.
  2. Check the results using long division.

Solution

  1. Write the terms of the dividend in order of descending power. Then, write just the coefficients of the dividend. Use zero for the coefficient of any missing powers. To the left, write the value of `-3` from the factor `x-3`. This is synthetic division by subtraction.

    The same process can be accomplished using synthetic division by addition. To the left of coefficients of the dividend, use the root of the divisor. In this case, the divisor is `x-3`, the root of which is `3`.

    The result can be written in the form of division statement.

    `(x^3-6x^2+7x+6)/(x-3)=x^2-3x-2`

    or

    `x^3-6x^2+7x+6=(x-3)(x^2-3x-2)`

    Since division by zero is not defined, the divisor cannot be zero: `x-3\ne 0` or `x\ne 3`.

  2. Long division check:

    The result is the same.

Example 3

Use the remainder theorem to determine the remainder when each polynomial is divided by `x-2`.

  1. `x^2-5x-3`
  2. `2x^2+x-10`
  3. `x^3+2x^2-8x+1`
  4. `3x^3-12x-10`

Solution

  1. `P(2)=(2)^2-5(2)-3`
    `=4-10-3`
    `=-9`

    The remainder is -9.

  2. `P(2)=2(2)^2+(2)-10`
    `=8+2-10`
    `=0`

    The remainder is 0.

  3. `P(2)=(2)^3+2(2)^2-8(2)+1`
    `=8+8-16+1`
    `=1`

    The remainder is 1.

  4. `P(2)=3(2)^3-12(2)-10`
    `=24-24-10`
    `=-10`

    The remainder is -10.

Example 4

Use the remainder theorem to determine the remainder when `2x^3-x^2-4x-4` is divided by each binomial.

  1. `x-1`
  2. `x+1`
  3. `x-2`
  4. `x+3`

Solution

  1. `P(1)=2(1)^3-(1)^2-4(1)-4`
    `=2-1-4-4`
    `=-7`

    The remainder is -7.

  2. `P(-1)=2(-1)^3-(-1)^2-4(-1)-4`
    `=-2-1+4-4`
    `=-3`

    The remainder is -3.

  3. `P(2)=2(2)^3-(2)^2-4(2)-4`
    `=16-4-8-4`
    `=0`

    The remainder is 0.

  4. `P(-3)=2(-3)^3-(-3)^2-4(-3)-4`
    `=-54-9+12-4`
    `=-55`

    The remainder is -55.

Example 8

An electronic message must be sent with a generated polynomial code

`P(x)=x^2+2x+1`

so that it may be verified by a receiver. The basis for the verification is obtaining the remainder of a division by a binomial key, `x+k`, `k>0`. Find the `k`-value in the binomial key when the remainder is 100.

Solution

The remainder theorem states that, when `P(x)=x^2+2x+1` is divided by `x+k`, the remainder is `P(-k)`.

`P(-k)=100`

`(-k)^2+2(-k)+1=100`

`k^2-2k-99=0`

`(k-11)(k+9)=2`

`k=11` or `k=-9`.

The positive `k`-value is 11.

Example 9

Write a polynomial that satisfies each set of conditions.

  1. A quadratic polynomial that gives a remainder of -4 when it is divided by `x-1`.
  2. A cubic polynomial that gives a remainder of 3 when it is divided by `x+2`.
  3. A quartic polynomial that gives a remainder of 1 when it is divided by `x+1`.

Solution

  1. Answers may vary.

    Since the dividend is of degree 2 and the divisor is of degree 1, you know that the quotient must be of degree 2 minus 1 or 1. For example, the quotient is `x+2`. The polynomial can be found by multiplication and then addition.

    `(x-1)(x+2)+(-4)=x^2+x-6`

  2. Answers may vary.

    Since the dividend is of degree 3 and the divisor is of degree 1, you know that the quotient must be of degree 3 minus 1 or 2. For example, the quotient is `x^2+2x-1`. The polynomial can be found by multiplication and then addition.

    `(x+2)(x^2+2x-1)+3=x^3+4x^2+3x+1`

  3. Answers may vary.

    Since the dividend is of degree 4 and the divisor is of degree 1, you know that the quotient must be of degree 4 minus 1 or 3. For example, the quotient is `x^3+2x^2-x-1`. The polynomial can be found by multiplication and then addition.

    `(x+1)(x^3+2x^2-x-1)+1=x^4+3x^3+x^2-2x`
